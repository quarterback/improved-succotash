Good. Now we stay concrete.

You need three data pipelines. Not theory. Actual sources you can pull.

⸻

1. HUMAN COST (your baseline)

This is the cleanest part.

You map workloads → occupations → cost per unit.

Primary sources:
	•	Bureau of Labor Statistics (OEWS)
	•	Wage + employment for ~800 occupations  ￼
	•	Available by industry, geography, and occupation
	•	Employer Costs for Employee Compensation (ECEC)
	•	Adds benefits + overhead (this matters more than wages alone)  ￼
	•	National Compensation Survey (NCS)
	•	Gives fully loaded compensation structures  ￼

What this gives you:
	•	$/hour by occupation
	•	employment counts
	•	industry context

What you still have to do:
	•	convert to cost per workload unit

Example:
	•	“claims classification” → SOC: claims adjusters
	•	wage → hourly → time per task → cost per unit

This is modeling work, not data scarcity.

⸻

2. PROCUREMENT + SPEND (this is your leverage)

This is where your index becomes real.

Primary sources:
	•	Federal Procurement Data System
	•	Full contract-level data: who bought what, from whom, for how much  ￼
	•	USAspending (front-end to FPDS)
	•	Agency budget documents (line items + program spend)
	•	State equivalents (often even cleaner than federal)

Also:
	•	Private aggregators (Fedmine / GovSpend) combine ~19 federal datasets  ￼

What this gives you:
	•	contractor labor spend by category
	•	pre vs post AI procurement signals
	•	vendor shift (Accenture → OpenAI wrapper, etc.)

This is how you observe:
	•	substitution in dollars before headcount

⸻

3. HEADCOUNT + ABSORPTION (hardest, but tractable)

This is the part people think is impossible. It’s not, but it’s messy.

Primary sources:
	•	Office of Personnel Management
	•	Federal employee salary + staffing data  ￼
	•	BLS datasets:
	•	QCEW → employment by industry  ￼
	•	JOLTS → hiring, quits, layoffs
	•	Agency staffing plans / budget justifications
	•	Union reports (underrated, very specific about role changes)

Augment with:
	•	Job postings (NLx / scraped postings)
	•	RFP language shifts (what roles agencies think they need now)

What this gives you:
	•	hiring freezes vs growth
	•	contractor → internal shifts
	•	role reclassification (this is key signal)

⸻

4. TASK / WORKLOAD MAPPING (this is your glue layer)

You cannot skip this.

You need a translation layer:

workload → task → occupation → cost

Sources:
	•	SOC system (standard occupation mapping)  ￼
	•	O*NET / ORS (task-level breakdowns)  ￼

This is where you define:
	•	what “classification” actually is
	•	what “judgment” splits into
	•	what “summarization” maps to in labor terms

Without this, your index collapses into vibes.

⸻

How the formula actually gets computed

Per workload category:

1. Substitution rate

You don’t measure this nationally.

You derive it from:
	•	workflow changes (RFPs, internal docs)
	•	vendor replacement (services → AI tools)
	•	time-on-task reduction (case studies, audits)

Proxy version:
	•	% reduction in contractor spend for that task
	•	% increase in AI-related procurement for same function

⸻

2. Cost displacement

This is the cleanest equation:

Displacement = (human cost per unit × volume) − (AI cost per unit × volume)

You already own the AI side.

Human side comes from:
	•	BLS wages
	•	time-per-task estimates

⸻

3. Absorption rate

This is not one metric. It’s a classification problem.

For each observed unit:
	•	eliminated
	•	reallocated internally
	•	upgraded to higher-skill work
	•	converted to contractor reduction
	•	frozen (no backfill)

You infer this from:
	•	headcount trends
	•	hiring vs attrition
	•	budget shifts

⸻

Where people mess this up (don’t do this)
	1.	They try to start national
You don’t. You start with 5–10 agencies where data is legible.
	2.	They treat wages as cost
Wrong. Use fully loaded compensation or you understate displacement.
	3.	They skip procurement
That’s the strongest signal you have. It shows intent before outcomes.
	4.	They generalize “AI usage”
You only track specific workloads tied to dollars.

⸻

What your first usable dataset actually looks like

Not big. Tight.

Pick:
	•	SNAP eligibility processing
	•	unemployment claims
	•	call center triage
	•	document summarization in casework

Then build:
	•	baseline labor cost per unit
	•	current AI cost per unit
	•	procurement change
	•	staffing change

That’s enough to publish something real.

⸻

Bottom line

You already have the hardest piece (AI cost).

Everything else exists, but it’s fragmented:
	•	BLS → labor cost + structure
	•	FPDS / budgets → where money moved
	•	OPM / QCEW → what happened to people

Your job is not finding data.

Your job is forcing these into the same unit of analysis: workload.
