Labor Displacement Index

A workload-level index measuring the cost differential between human and AI execution across government and enterprise functions. Derived from BLS compensation data, federal procurement records, and OPM staffing signals — forced into a single unit of analysis: cost per workload.

Measurement Pillars

Three data streams forced into a common unit — cost per workload. Each pillar has a distinct source, a distinct lag, and a distinct failure mode.

Contract-level federal spend by category. Pre vs. post AI procurement signals. Substitution visible in dollars before it appears in headcount.

OPM · QCEW · JOLTS

Eliminated · reallocated · upgraded · frozen. Inferred from headcount trends, hiring vs. attrition, and budget shifts — not self-reported.

Core Formula

Cost Displacement

Displacement = (human_cost_per_unit × volume) − (ai_cost_per_unit × volume)

AI cost per unit is already tracked by the Compute CPI. Human cost per unit is derived from BLS OEWS wages + ECEC benefits overhead, divided by time-per-task estimates from O*NET task-level data. Volume is observed from QCEW employment counts and FPDS contract activity.

Substitution Rate (Proxy)

sub_rate ≈ (Δ contractor_spend[task] / baseline_spend) + (Δ AI_procurement[task] / baseline_spend)

Not measured nationally. Derived per workload category from FPDS contract-level data: % reduction in contractor spend for a task type, offset against % increase in AI-related procurement for the same function. Intent shows in dollars before outcomes show in headcount.

Data Sources

Four pipelines. All public. None designed to answer this question — which is why the workload translation layer is the actual work.

Pipeline 1 · Human Cost
BLS Labor Data
  • OEWS — wages + employment for ~800 occupations, by industry and geography
  • ECEC — benefits + overhead on top of wages (fully loaded compensation)
  • NCS — full compensation structures for cross-validation
Output: $/hour by SOC code → converted to cost per workload unit via O*NET time-on-task estimates
Pipeline 2 · Procurement Signal
Federal Spend Data
  • FPDS — contract-level data: who bought what, from whom, for how much
  • USAspending — front-end to FPDS; agency budget line items
  • Fedmine / GovSpend — aggregators combining ~19 federal datasets
Output: contractor labor spend by task category; pre/post AI procurement signals; vendor shift detection
Pipeline 3 · Headcount & Absorption
OPM + BLS Staffing
  • OPM — federal employee salary and staffing data
  • QCEW — quarterly employment by industry (BLS)
  • JOLTS — hiring, quits, and layoffs by sector
  • NLx / job postings — role language shifts in RFPs and listings
Output: hiring freeze vs. growth; contractor → internal shifts; role reclassification signals
Pipeline 4 · Workload Translation
SOC / O*NET Mapping
  • SOC system — standard occupation classification (anchor for all labor data)
  • O*NET / ORS — task-level breakdowns by occupation
The glue layer. Translates workload → task → occupation → cost. Without this, the index collapses. This is where "classification" becomes a SOC code and a time estimate.

Methodology Notes

Use fully loaded compensation. BLS wages alone understate true human cost by 30–40%. The Employer Costs for Employee Compensation (ECEC) adds benefits, payroll taxes, and overhead. Using wages only means systematically understating displacement.

Procurement is the lead indicator. Federal contract data shows intent before outcomes appear in headcount. A vendor shift from a staffing firm to an AI tool wrapper shows in FPDS quarters before anyone's job title changes. This is the strongest signal available.

Only specific workloads tied to dollars. This index does not generalize "AI usage." It tracks measurable cost differentials for defined task categories. If a workload cannot be anchored to a SOC code, an FPDS contract category, and a time-per-unit estimate, it is not tracked.

Scope is intentionally narrow. Initial coverage: 5–10 federal agencies where procurement and staffing data are most legible. National extrapolation follows once workload costs are validated at the agency level.

Relationship to Compute CPI

The Compute CPI tracks AI cost deflation — what it costs to run AI workloads across model tiers. The LDI uses that data as the AI-side input to the displacement formula. As AI costs fall (CPI declines), displacement widens, all else equal.
